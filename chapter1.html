Chapter 1 — Introduction

1.1 Statement of the Research Problem

Achieving Universal Health Coverage (UHC) requires that all individuals access essential healthcare services without suffering financial hardship. However, in many low-income countries, including Malawi, households continue to face substantial out-of-pocket (OOP) payments when seeking healthcare. These payments can be unpredictable and burdensome, leading to catastrophic health expenditures (CHE) that divert resources from basic needs and, in extreme cases, push households into poverty.

In Malawi, healthcare is officially provided free at the point of care in public facilities, yet significant costs remain due to medicine stock-outs, transport expenses, and the use of private and mission facilities where user fees are charged. The health financing landscape is heavily donor-dependent, with external funding accounting for over 50% of total health expenditure, while households themselves contribute about 12% through OOP spending. Such a structure leaves households vulnerable, especially as donor funding becomes less predictable and domestic resource mobilization remains limited.

Findings from a study conducted between 2019 and 2020 on the uptake of health insurance in Malawi revealed that only 1% of the Malawian population was covered by health insurance, with 47% of the insured population obtaining coverage through their employers (Ng’ambi et al., 2023). This leaves a substantial portion of the population vulnerable to financial strain from healthcare costs. Furthermore, disparities in coverage between urban and rural areas are evident, with urban residents more likely to have access to insurance schemes compared to their rural counterparts, despite approximately 84% of the Malawian population residing in rural areas (McBride & Moucheraud, 2022). With the absence of social health insurance schemes and out-of-pocket expenditures accounting for 11.9% of total health expenditure, a significant proportion of Malawi's population remains at risk of financial hardship and impoverishment due to excessive medical expenses.

Previous studies in Malawi (e.g., Mchenga et al., 2017; Mulaga et al., 2021) have established the presence of CHE and its impoverishing effects. However, apart from a recent illness-specific study on sickle cell anemia by Kamya et al. (2025) who found that malaria episodes are a major driver of catastrophic health expenditures in Malawi and Uganda, most studies have focused primarily on broad socioeconomic and demographic determinants as well as national estimates of catastrophic health expenditures and impoverishment incidence. Further, the study on Malawi and Uganda mentioned above focused on disease specific evidence for one illness; sickle cell anemia, not nationally representative illness disaggregated analysis. Thus, there remains limited understanding of how nationally representative illness-specific factors, health-seeking behaviors, and healthcare provider choices influence financial risk exposure. The distribution of catastrophic health expenditures and impoverishment estimates across households for various illnesses and health seeking behaviors is also less explored. In addition, there lacks updated evidence on incidence and poverty implications of catastrophic health expenditures that is necessary given changing disease patterns, rising chronic illness burdens, and ongoing health financing reforms, such as those outlined in the Malawi Health Financing Strategy 2023–2030 and Health Sector Strategic Plan III (HSSP III). This is the knowledge gap this study seeks to fill.

1.2 Research Questions

  1. What are the determinants of catastrophic health expenditures in Malawi?
  2. What is the incidence, intensity and distribution of catastrophic health expenditures across households in Malawi?
  3. What is the incidence, intensity and distribution of poverty due to out-of-pocket expenditures across households in Malawi?

1.3 Objective of the Study

The main objective of this study is to identify the determinants of catastrophic health expenditures and estimate the extent of impoverishment due to out-of-pocket expenditures across households in Malawi.

  1. To identify the determinants of catastrophic health expenditures in Malawi.
  2. To estimate the incidence, intensity, and distribution of catastrophic health expenditures across households in Malawi.
  3. To assess the incidence, intensity and distribution of poverty due to out-of-pocket expenditures across households in Malawi.

1.4 Research Hypotheses

  1. The determinants of catastrophic health expenditures do not significantly affect Catastrophic health expenditures in Malawi.
  2. Out-of-pocket expenditures do not significantly affect the incidence, intensity and distribution of Catastrophic health expenditures across households in Malawi.
  3. Out-of-pocket expenditures do not significantly affect the incidence, intensity and distribution of poverty in Malawi.

1.5 Significance of the Study

This study makes an important contribution to the literature on health financing and financial risk protection in low-income countries, with a particular focus on Malawi. While previous studies on catastrophic health expenditure (CHE) in Malawi (e.g., Mchenga et al., 2017; Mulaga et al., 2021) have provided valuable insights into the incidence and determinants of CHE, they have generally been limited to aggregate socioeconomic and demographic factors. This study advances existing knowledge in several unique ways.

First, it uses nationally representative data of 11,434 households and applies logistic regression to examine the determinants of CHE, integrating not only socioeconomic and demographic variables but also illness-specific factors and health-seeking behaviours. This approach provides a more detailed understanding of how disease profiles and provider choice influence financial risk, an area largely unexplored in previous Malawi-focused studies.

Second, the study estimates both the incidence, intensity and distribution of CHE across multiple thresholds (10%, 15%, 25%, 40%) and expenditure definitions (total versus non-food expenditure). By doing so, it provides a detailed picture of financial risk distribution under varying policy-relevant assumptions, improving comparability with global and regional benchmarks.

Third, the study examines the poverty implications, also known as the impoverishing effects, of out-of-pocket (OOP) healthcare payments on household poverty and poverty gaps, offering updated estimates of how health expenditures push households into poverty or deepen their existing poverty. This is critical for informing Malawi’s current health financing reforms, including the Health Financing Strategy and the Health Sector Strategic Plan III (HSSP III), which emphasize financial risk protection as a pathway to Universal Health Coverage (UHC).

Finally, the study provides evidence-based recommendations to guide policy action. It highlights vulnerable populations, such as households with chronic illness, dependents (children under five and elderly members), and those using private or mission/CHAM facilities, that require targeted financial protection mechanisms. These insights are directly relevant for policymakers seeking to design equitable health financing strategies, reduce OOP burden, and build a resilient health system.

1.6 Scope and Delimitation of the Study

This study focused on examining the determinants, distribution and impoverishing effects of catastrophic health expenditures (CHE) in Malawi using nationally representative data from 11,434 households. The analysis employed multiple thresholds of CHE (10%, 15%, 25%, and 40% of total and non-food expenditure) to provide a thorough understanding of financial risk exposure under different policy-relevant definitions. The determinants considered include household socioeconomic and demographic characteristics, health status (including chronic illness and illness-specific conditions), and health-seeking behaviours such as facility choice.

The study also assessed the impact of out-of-pocket (OOP) health expenditures on household poverty, measuring changes in the poverty headcount and poverty gap before and after health spending. Logistic regression model was applied to identify significant predictors of CHE, while impoverishment analysis on policy implications employed standard poverty measurement techniques. Findings were contextualized within Malawi’s evolving health financing landscape, including the Malawi Health Financing Strategy and the Health Sector Strategic Plan III (HSSP III).

While this study offers valuable insights, certain delimitations are worth noting. The analysis is based on cross-sectional household survey data, which limits the ability to capture long-term coping strategies adopted by households following health expenditure shocks. In addition, the household expenditure data, including OOP health payments, relied on respondent recall, which may be subject to measurement error and recall bias.

The study did not disaggregate health insurance types or benefit packages in detail as that data was not available; hence, findings related to insurance and CHE reflect general patterns rather than scheme-specific impacts. Finally, while the study incorporated illness-specific and behavioural factors, it did not explicitly examine clinical outcomes or quality of care, which could also influence health-seeking behaviour and financial risk exposure.

In spite of these limitations, the study provides comprehensive and policy-relevant evidence on financial risk protection in Malawi and highlights priority areas for health financing reforms.
