Abstract

In Malawi, households contribute about 12% of Total Health Expenditure through out-of-pocket payments (OOP). Such a financing structure leaves households vulnerable leading to financial hardship resulting from Catastrophic Health Expenditures (CHE). The aim of the study was to identify determinants of Catastrophic Health Expenditures and estimate the distribution and extent of impoverishment due to out-of-pocket expenditures across households in Malawi.

Secondary data from a nationally representative survey of 11,434 households in the 2019–2020 Integrated Household Survey (IHS5) was used. Data analysis involved both Descriptive and Regression analysis. Descriptive analysis utilized percentages to assess the incidence and intensity of Catastrophic Health Expenditures, along with their impoverishing effects. CHE thresholds of 10%, 15%, 25%, and 40% of total and non-food expenditure were used. Logistic regression was used to identified determinants of CHE at the 40% non-food threshold.

Results showed CHE incidence ranging from 26.0% at the 10% threshold to 1.7% at the 40% non-food threshold. The mean overshoot was 6.33%, indicating that among households incurring CHE, health spending exceeded the threshold by an average of 6.33% of non-food expenditure. Richer households were significantly protected from financial hardship. Rural households, those with young children, elderly members (65+) and female-headed households were more vulnerable. Households which had members with chronic illness were at greater risk of financial hardship.

Unexpected results showed that insured households were at a high risk of incurring CHE. Importantly, households with members suffering from illnesses such as hypertension, body and joint pains, fever/malaria, sore throat, stomach ache, skin problems, wound and other chronic conditions had significantly higher odds of incurring CHE, while health-seeking behaviors had the highest effects: households using mission/CHAM facilities (AOR = 7.69, 95% CI: 5.52–10.72, p < 0.001), or private facilities (AOR = 4.11, 95% CI: 3.27–5.16, p < 0.001) were far more likely to incur CHE compared to those that did not use each service. Households that used informal commercial sources had lower risk of CHE.

Out-of-pocket spending pushed additional 0.7% of Malawian population into poverty and pushed already poor people further down into poverty by 1.4%. More cases of a particular illness in a household as well as more of a particular health seeking behavior were generally associated with higher CHE and impoverishment estimates. In conclusion, the study identified sociodemographic, illness and care seeking factors as determinants of catastrophic health expenditures in Malawi hence the need for financial risk protection policies.
