Chapter two — Background of the Study

2.1 Introduction

This chapter discusses the structure and organization of the Malawi healthcare system. It further explores the country's health financing system regarding how resources are mobilized, how risk is spread, and how healthcare services are purchased. It also examines the challenges faced by the health financing system and the efforts and policy reforms implemented to address these challenges. It concludes by discussing the policy reforms outlined in the Health Sector Strategic Plan III (HSSP III), 2023-2030.

2.2 Structure of Malawi Healthcare System and Health Service Delivery

The backbone of healthcare services delivery in Malawi is the government, which administers services through the Ministry of Health (MOH). Throughout the country, each district boasts public hospitals that offer services free of charge at the point of care. Following closely in scale is the not-for-profit Christian Health Association of Malawi (CHAM), consisting of church-affiliated health facilities. CHAM facilities operate with a focus on community well-being rather than profit. The Islamic Health Association of Malawi (IHAM), though smaller in size compared to CHAM, also plays a role in providing healthcare services. There are, also, scattered independent private health facilities across the nation, operating on either a for-profit or not-for-profit basis. (Health Account Report, 2020)

2.3 Organization of Malawi Healthcare System

The health system in Malawi has three levels: primary healthcare, secondary healthcare, and tertiary healthcare. A referral system links together these three tiers. (MoH, 2020)

2.3.1 Primary Healthcare

In Malawi, health centers and community hospitals are distributed across the country, and they constitute the majority of primary healthcare service providers. Only 39.4% of Total health expenditures is allocated to primary healthcare which is low, given that financing primary healthcare is important to ensuring financial risk protection and achieving universal health coverage as it ensures allocative efficiency.

2.3.2 Secondary Healthcare

The secondary healthcare tier comprises district hospitals that are publicly owned. There is a total of 21 district hospitals located in various health districts in the country. Some secondary healthcare facilities are also provided by the Christian Health Association of Malawi (CHAM) in a Public-Private Partnership with the government. In Malawi, secondary healthcare facilities also provide primary healthcare services and that explains the low funding for primary care as some primary care funds are channeled to secondary care facilities to provide primary care services. (MoH, 2023)

2.3.3 Tertiary Healthcare

Tertiary healthcare services are provided by central hospitals. There are four central hospitals located in the cities of Zomba, Lilongwe, Blantyre and Mzuzu; Zomba Central Hospital, the Kamuzu Central Hospital, the Queen Elizabeth Central Hospital and the Mzuzu Central Hospital respectively.

2.4 Malawi Healthcare Financing System

At the heart of the Malawi healthcare system lies the Malawi health financing system acting as the driving force of all health interventions strategized by the Ministry of Health.

2.4.1 The Goal of the Financing System

As indicated in the Malawi Health Financing Strategy 2023-2030, the goal of the Malawi health financing system is to mobilize and distribute adequate resources, efficiently and equitably, and to purchase services strategically based on a well-defined health benefit package to achieve Universal health coverage goals. (MoH, 2023). This aligns with the National health policy goal adopted by the Malawi Health Sector Strategic Plan III, 2023-2030. The goal is to improve the health status of all Malawians and increase the satisfaction of clients as well as financial risk protection towards achieving universal health coverage.

2.4.2 Structure and Functions of the System

The health financing system in Malawi is a core function of the Malawi healthcare system. It is the channel to progress toward Universal health coverage through improving service coverage and financial risk protection. The system performs the three traditional health financing functions of resource mobilization, pooling, and purchasing of healthcare services. It however also performs a fourth function of health benefit package design (MoH, 2023).

2.4.3 Resource Mobilization

The financing structure of Malawi's healthcare system relies on multiple sources of funds, including donors (external financing), private health expenditures (household out-of-pocket expenditure, medical insurance, and other corporate funds), and the government through the Ministry of Finance. The government utilizes solidarity in financing health through funds collected via taxation. These funds are directed through financial agents, encompassing government bodies, non-governmental organizations, and private organizations (Zere et al, 2010). These mandatory contributions are effective as far as financial risk protection and universal health coverage are concerned since they encourage cross-subsidization from the rich to the poor. Donor contributions constitute a significant portion of Malawi's Total Health Expenditure (THE). According to the National Health Financing Strategy 2023-2030 (MoH, 2023), total health expenditure is made up of 24.1% from government contributions, 54.5% from external financing, 11.9% from out-of-pocket expenditures, and 9.1% from private healthcare insurance.

2.4.5 Domestic Health Resource Mobilization Strategies in Malawi

i. Introduction of optional paying services: The Ministry of Health stepped up optional paying services in central hospitals and further piloted paying wards in two secondary care hospitals: Nkhotakota and Nkhata-Bay. This was done to increase the resource mobilization capacities of hospitals. With this arrangement, all patients continue to access the free services provided by the public hospitals. However, those patients able and willing to pay for extra-benefit-package services, are offered the services at a fee. (MoH, 2023)

ii. Public-Private Partnerships (PPP): To augment levels of domestic health financing the government put together a policy for Public-Private partnerships to encourage the private sector’s participation in healthcare financing. The Health Sector Strategic Plan recognizes this as an avenue to improve health equipment and infrastructure. According to National Health Accounts reports, while Total health expenditure has been increasing, capital expenditure has been relatively constant as shown in Figure 2 below. (MoH, 2020). This low capital investment is one contributor to low-quality services in the country. Apart from enhancing the quality and efficiency of existing healthcare infrastructures, Public-Private Partnerships are key to upgrading the quantity of the infrastructures. As of 2020, the Public-Private Partnership between the Christian Association of Malawi (CHAM) and the Malawi government is the only PPP in force, and it is on services provision. (Tobias, 2020)

2.4.6 Resource Pooling

Resource pooling involves grouping collected healthcare resources to spread financial risk across the population. It prevents the poor from carrying the full burden of paying for healthcare on their own. Resource pooling enables cross-subsidization from the rich to the poor, from the sick to the healthy, and from the young to the old.

i. The Health Service Joint Fund (HSJF): A multi-donor pooling arrangement established by the Malawi government and its development partners to support the country’s health financing system. Set up in 2015, the fund’s objective is to pool, coordinate, and channel donor resources to address priority health service constraints in Malawi. (MoH, 2017)

ii. Private health insurance: Pooling resources via private health insurance in Malawi contributes only a small share of pooling efforts in the health financing system as the health insurance market in Malawi is in its youthful stage comprising only one major firm called the Malawi Aid Society of Malawi (MASM) followed by a few other medical schemes that are in their infancy. (MASM, 2024; Makoka, Kaluwa & Kambewa, 2007)

iii. Social Health Insurance: The effective implementation of social health insurance in Malawi is a challenge mainly because the formal sector from which most of the premiums are to be collected is narrow (World Bank Group, 2017). Strategies to include the informal sector are outlined in the Health Sector Strategic Plan III (MoH, 2023)

2.4.7 Challenges of the Health Financing System

Some challenges include inadequate government funding, the lateness of government funding, a decline in donor funding, inefficiency and inequity in resource pooling, and weakness in the income generation capacity of hospitals and councils. (Hanif & Musvoto, 2023)

2.4.8 Efforts and Policy Reforms: HSSP III, 2023-2030

The Ministry of Health has outlined strategies to improve resource mobilization, pooling, and healthcare purchasing while reshaping the system to eliminate inefficiencies. Strategies include increasing government budget allocation, implementing paying services at all levels, community-based resource mobilization, promotion of private health financing, efficiency and equity in pooling, strategic purchasing, and rationing health benefits.
