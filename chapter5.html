5.5 Discussion of Findings

The analysis shows that wealth, insurance coverage, presence of young children and elderly members, certain illnesses, and choice of health facility were significant predictors of CHE. Higher wealth quintiles and urban residence reduced CHE risk, whereas having insurance, chronic illness, young children, elderly members, and using mission or private facilities increased CHE likelihood. Education, sex of household head, and household size were largely non-significant.

Out-of-pocket spending pushed 1.7% of households into financial hardship, while 0.7% were newly impoverished and 1.4% of the already poor became deeper in poverty. Vulnerable households—those with young children, elderly, or chronically ill members—experienced disproportionate financial risk. Insured households faced unexpectedly higher CHE risk, possibly due to adverse selection or limited coverage.

Illness-specific and health-seeking behaviour factors strongly influenced CHE. Chronic and recurrent conditions such as hypertension, body and joint pains, malaria, sore throat, stomach ache, and wounds were major cost drivers. Households using mission/CHAM or private facilities incurred higher CHE, reflecting higher user fees and OOP costs.

5.6 Policy Implications

  • Expand prepayment and risk-pooling mechanisms, including community-based and national health insurance, with subsidies for the poor.
  • Strengthen health system responses to communicable and non-communicable diseases, focusing on prevention, early detection, and primary care management.
  • Reduce financial burden in mission/CHAM and private facilities via better subsidies, service-level agreements, and price regulation.
  • Target vulnerable households (with children under five or elderly members) with grants, maternal and child health programs, and social protection.
  • Review insurance benefit packages and reimbursement systems to ensure genuine financial protection.

These policy actions are essential to reduce financial risk from health spending and advance Malawi’s Universal Health Coverage (UHC) and Sustainable Development Goal 3.8.
