Chapter 4 — Methodology

4.1 Introduction

This chapter outlines the theoretical framework and methodology for the study. The sections following this introduction cover the theoretical framework adopted, the method used for robustness checks, and the model specification for the determinants of catastrophic health expenditures. Estimating the incidence and intensity of catastrophic health expenditures and impoverishment, as discussed in the Theoretical Framework section, is crucial. It will highlight the extent of financial hardship associated with out-of-pocket expenditures in Malawi. Understanding the determinants of these catastrophic health expenditures, as presented in the model specification section, will also guide policy direction. Finally, the chapter discusses the data sources that will be used in the study.

4.2 Theoretical Framework

This study adopts the Grossman Model of Health Capital Demand (Grossman, 1972) as the primary theoretical lens to examine the determinants of catastrophic health expenditures (CHE) at the household level. The Grossman model conceptualizes health not merely as a consumption good that yields direct utility but also as an investment good that enhances individuals’ productivity, longevity, and economic potential (Grossman, 2000). In this framework, health is treated as a form of capital stock that depreciates naturally over time but can be augmented or maintained through investments such as medical care, proper nutrition, safe water, sanitation, and healthy lifestyles (Mushkin, 1962; Muurinen, 1982).

At the heart of the Grossman model is the assumption that households act as rational agents who seek to maximize lifetime utility subject to income and time constraints. The utility function depends on both health status and the consumption of other goods, and decisions about health investments involve comparing the marginal benefits of improved health with the costs of health inputs. These investments draw on both direct financial resources and time inputs and are influenced by household preferences, information, and the structure of the surrounding health system (Grossman, 1972; Wagstaff, 1986). When illness strikes, particularly chronic conditions or severe acute episodes, the depreciation of health capital accelerates, prompting greater health investment needs. Yet, under financial constraints, households often face difficult trade-offs. Some delay treatment or resort to lower-quality care, while others are forced to mobilize significant financial resources, sometimes exceeding sustainable levels. Such events may culminate in catastrophic health expenditure, defined by the World Health Organization as health spending surpassing a critical share of household resources (e.g., ≥10% of total expenditure or ≥40% of non-food expenditure) (Wagstaff et al., 2003; Xu et al., 2007).

The Grossman framework explains why vulnerability to CHE is systematically linked to household characteristics and behaviour. Households with higher income and better education tend to have greater capacity to finance health care and are more efficient in producing and maintaining health (Cutler & Lleras-Muney, 2010). Education improves health literacy, enabling households to adopt preventive measures and make informed choices about when and where to seek care. Conversely, lower-income and less-educated households may delay or forgo preventive care, increasing the risk of more severe health episodes and higher costs when care is eventually sought (Gottret & Schieber, 2006). The effective price of health inputs, which includes not only user fees but also travel costs and time lost from work, further influences these decisions. For instance, households living far from health facilities incur higher indirect costs, which can deter timely care and exacerbate health shocks when care finally becomes unavoidable (Garchitorena et al., 2017).

Health insurance, where present, mitigates these risks by lowering the effective price of health care, enabling households to maintain health capital without incurring prohibitive out-of-pocket (OOP) costs (Ekman, 2004; Nguyen et al., 2012). In its absence, households rely more heavily on direct payments at the point of service, making them susceptible to financial distress when illness episodes are severe or frequent. Household demographics also matter: larger households or those with many dependents, such as children or elderly members, face higher illness exposure and more competing demands on income and time, increasing their vulnerability to CHE (Buigut et al., 2015).

Health status and illness burden are critical in this framework, as chronic illnesses, recurring symptoms, and multiple comorbidities reduce the stock of health capital and necessitate repeated or prolonged investments in care (Odhiambo et al., 2020). These health conditions, represented in this study by reported disease symptoms such as backache, blood pressure problems, respiratory infections, malaria, and others, directly translate into increased health care demand and expenditures. Health-seeking behaviour, moreover, influences both the magnitude and pattern of spending. Households that opt for private or mission facilities, or that rely on informal and traditional care, face different cost structures and treatment outcomes, which in turn shape financial risk (Kruk et al., 2009). In the Grossman context, these behaviours reflect household preferences and constraints in the allocation of resources for health production, reinforcing the idea that CHE is not merely the product of random shocks but of systematic factors rooted in health needs, health behaviours, and economic capacity.

Thus, the Grossman model provides a robust theoretical foundation for this study. It links socioeconomic status, insurance coverage, household composition, illness burden, and health-seeking behaviour to patterns of health care use and financial risk. This integrated perspective justifies the inclusion of variables capturing not only socioeconomic and demographic factors but also reported disease conditions and treatment choices, ensuring that the empirical analysis accounts for both the demand for health care and the economic consequences of healthcare utilization. CHE, in this view, is an outcome of household-level production and consumption decisions under health shocks and financial constraints, rather than a purely random financial event.

4.3 Model Specification

This study utilizes a logistic regression model to explore the characteristics of households related to Catastrophic health expenditures. Based on Xu (2005), the functional form of the model to estimate the determinants of Catastrophic health expenditures is expressed as:

y = α + Σβi Xi
y = ln (p/(1-p))

where:
y is a dummy variable on catastrophic health expenditure, taking the value of 1 if the household experiences catastrophic health expenditure and 0 otherwise.
α is the constant,
βi is the coefficient of the independent variable Xi
Xi are the independent variables.

The explanatory variables considered include broad categories such as:

  • Socioeconomic and demographic factors: household insurance status, location (urban/rural), education of household head, household size, socioeconomic status, household age structure, age and gender of household head.
  • Health condition indicators: presence of chronic illness; number of individuals with disease burdens such as backache, blood pressure, body and joint pains, cough, dental problem, diarrhoea, eye problem, fever/malaria, headache, heart problem, other illnesses, respiratory infection, skin problem, sore throat, stomach ache, and wound.
  • Health-seeking behavior: treatment choices such as doing nothing, government facility, informal commercial sources, mission and CHAM facility, private facility, or traditional and personal remedies.

p is the probability of a household facing catastrophic health expenditure.

4.4 Data Sources

The study utilized secondary data collected by the National Statistical Office (NSO) of Malawi between April 2019 and April 2020 under the Fifth Integrated Household Survey (IHS5). The IHS surveys are conducted roughly every 3 to 5 years and serve as a key instrument for the Government of Malawi to monitor changing household conditions. The IHS is also a vital tool for tracking progress toward achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and the objectives of the Malawi Growth and Development Strategy (MGDS).

At the time of this study, the IHS6 dataset had not yet been released; thus, the IHS5 represented the most recent and comprehensive nationally representative dataset available.

The IHS5 covered a wide range of topics relevant to this study, including: household demographics, education, health, consumption, food security, and durable goods. Importantly, the survey provides detailed information on household expenditures and health status, which are essential for the analysis of catastrophic health expenditures.

The survey employed a stratified two-stage sampling design based on the 2018 Population and Housing Census (PHC), with representativeness ensured at the national, regional, urban/rural, and district levels. The final achieved sample consisted of 12,288 households from 768 Enumeration Areas (EAs), with an overall response rate of 93 percent.
