Chapter 6 — Summary, Conclusions, and Recommendations

6.1 Introduction

This chapter presents a synthesis of key findings from the study on catastrophic health expenditure (CHE) and impoverishment in Malawi. It summarizes the main results, draws conclusions, and offers recommendations for policy, practice, and future research. The chapter also highlights the study’s contributions to knowledge and outlines its limitations.

6.2 Summary of Findings

This study found:

  1. Incidence and intensity of CHE:

    Approximately one in four households experienced CHE at the 10% threshold of total household expenditure. At the 40% non-food expenditure threshold, 1.7% of households incurred CHE, with a mean overshoot of 6.33%.

  2. Inequalities in CHE distribution:

    Female-headed, rural, and older-headed households experienced higher CHE risk. Households with children under five, elderly members, or chronic illness were particularly vulnerable.

  3. Socioeconomic disparities:

    Poorest households had highest CHE incidence (11.4%) vs. richest (3.4%). Low education and larger households were associated with higher CHE risk.

  4. Unexpected insurance effect:

    Insured households experienced higher CHE than uninsured, suggesting coverage limitations or adverse selection.

  5. Disease-specific financial risk:

    Households reporting hypertension, joint pain, malaria/fever, stomach ache, and other chronic illnesses had higher CHE odds.

  6. Health-seeking behavior and facility choice:

    Use of private and mission/CHAM facilities increased CHE risk, while informal sources lowered risk.

  7. Poverty and impoverishment:

    OOP health payments increased poverty headcount by 0.7% and deepened the poverty gap by 1.4%, particularly among vulnerable, rural, and low-education households.

6.3 Conclusions

CHE and impoverishment remain significant challenges in Malawi. Vulnerable groups, including female-headed, poor, less educated, large households, and those with children under five, elderly, or chronic illness, face disproportionate financial risk. Insurance coverage was unexpectedly associated with higher CHE, suggesting gaps in benefit coverage. Households using private or mission/CHAM facilities face higher financial risk. These findings highlight systemic gaps in Malawi’s health financing system.

6.4 Recommendations

Policy recommendations include:

  1. Strengthen prepayment and risk pooling mechanisms: Expand insurance coverage, benefit packages, and reduce OOP costs.
  2. Target vulnerable households: Subsidies or social protection for female-headed, elderly, children under five, chronic illness, and poorest quintiles.
  3. Address cost disparities: Regulate or subsidize private and mission/CHAM facilities.
  4. Enhance primary healthcare: Invest in rural infrastructure and workforce.
  5. Disease-specific protection: Subsidize medicines and management programs for chronic/acute illnesses.
  6. Promote preventive healthcare and literacy: Expand community health education and preventive measures.
  7. Evaluate insurance schemes: Assess and reform coverage gaps and benefits.
  8. Improve healthcare access and affordability: Invest in public-sector facilities, especially rural areas.
  9. Enhance preventive care: Strengthen public health campaigns to reduce chronic illness burden.

6.5 Contributions to Knowledge

  1. Updated national evidence of CHE and impoverishment in Malawi.
  2. Comprehensive assessment of incidence and intensity of CHE, and poverty metrics.
  3. Inclusion of illness-specific variables showing disease-specific CHE risk.
  4. Empirical examination of health-seeking behavior as a CHE determinant.
  5. Insurance paradox: evidence that insured households remain vulnerable to CHE.

6.6 Limitations of the Study

  1. Self-reported expenditure data may introduce recall bias.
  2. Lack of healthcare access and quality measures, including distance to facilities.
  3. Insurance variable lacks depth of coverage and co-payment details.
  4. Disease severity and treatment intensity unobserved.
  5. Potential reporting heterogeneity across populations.
