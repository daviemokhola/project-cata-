Table 3.1 above is a Literature Matrix summarizing the reviewed literature in this study. The reviewed literature demonstrates that catastrophic health expenditures (CHE) are influenced by a wide range of socioeconomic, demographic, and health-related factors. Generally, studies in Malawi and other low- and middle-income countries consistently show that household size, education level, income status, geographic location, and type of healthcare facility accessed play critical roles in determining financial risk protection outcomes. Households with elderly members, individuals living with chronic illnesses, or those utilizing mission and private facilities have been shown to experience a greater risk of CHE, while households with more working adults or access to social safety nets are often protected. These findings also extend to the impoverishing effects of out-of-pocket (OOP) healthcare payments, with studies confirming that OOP expenditures often increase both the poverty headcount and the poverty gap, pushing vulnerable households deeper into poverty.

3.6 Gaps in Literature

Although existing literature has advanced the understanding of the determinants and impoverishing effects of catastrophic health expenditures (CHE), important gaps remain. First, there is limited analysis within Malawi of illness-specific determinants of CHE and how health-seeking behaviors, including the use of private, mission/CHAM, or informal facilities, as well as the choice to forgo care entirely affect household financial risk protection. Most studies have focused on broad socioeconomic and demographic variables, leaving illness profiles and behavioral aspects largely unexplored despite their likely influence on healthcare costs.

Second, there is little recent evidence on how out-of-pocket (OOP) healthcare spending affects both the incidence and depth of poverty in Malawi, particularly within the context of evolving health sector reforms. The Malawi Health Financing Strategy (2023–2030) and Health Sector Strategic Plan III (HSSP III) emphasise financial risk protection and progress toward universal health coverage (UHC). However, empirical evidence quantifying the extent of CHE and poverty transitions under these new frameworks remains scarce.

This study addresses these gaps by integrating illness profiles and health-seeking behaviors into the analysis of CHE determinants, alongside traditional socioeconomic and demographic factors. It also provides updated, nationally representative evidence on the impoverishing effects of OOP healthcare spending. The findings are expected to inform policy decisions on sustainable health financing and improved financial protection, particularly in light of current health sector reforms and efforts to reduce donor dependency.
